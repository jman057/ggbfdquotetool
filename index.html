4. Private Time

An extended time at home, or an hour or two at the funeral home in the days before the service or committal, taking some private time is important for some families. This decision impacts transport arrangements and the funeral professional’s time.

5. Arranging a Service

There are any number of ways that families might arrange a Great Goodbye. Traditionally, the casket is present, and whether the service is formal or something less structured, this remains important for many. But families might choose to have the ashes instead of the casket, or opt for a memorial service at a later date.

6. Transport

Driving your loved one to the service and then on to the committal is a special way to get involved and personalise a Great Goodbye. For some people, taking this last drive together is deeply meaningful. If that’s not for you, your funeral professional will be honoured to do this task on your family’s behalf. If the elegance and formality of the large black hearse fits with your Great Goodbye, then ask your funeral director to arrange this. Increasingly, there is a wide range of vehicle options available from directors. A hearse is an additional charge to a standard director’s fee, usually upward of $450. But there is no law that says you can’t transport the casket yourself, and you might wish to step beyond historic convention and do something a little different. Grab the combi van they always took surfing or put the farm tractor to work for this final journey. Of course, if bidding farewell at home, you will not need to specify funeral transport beyond having the casket delivered to the crematorium or burial ground.
